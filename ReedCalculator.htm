Legere Oboe Reed - Is it worth it?

by Daniel Glynn

With Legere now offering a
Paid Amazon Link Quality Synthetic Oboe Reed,
professionals everywhere are taking a closer look at this high-end plastic alternative and its hefty $150 price point. In order to help the professional oboist make a more informed purchase, we went ahead and put together a few tools to determine one's actual reed-production cost, while also comparing this cost to any foreseeable savings from using the Legere Reed, given their advertised life expectancy.

"The Cost of One Cane Reed."

Taking into account all the factors that contribute to the cost of a traditional cane reed, the table below was built to total the "cost per reed" of items such as one's tube cane, thread, reed knife, and gouger. Notice how the "Total Cost" and subtotals below update upon entering one's individual costs of various reed-making materials. Since we're summing up the total cost of producing a reed from scratch, we've also factored in the reed-maker's labor which requires an hourly rate to be entered. Remember that there is no labor cost for the consumer when using a Legere Reed as this is essentially built in to the purchase price. Please note that when constructing the table below, the decision was made to exclude the cost of one's gouger(s) and shaper tip(s) as their inclusion would only slightly increase production cost per reed over a lifetime of reed-making.
As Legere boasts a life expectancy of several months per reed on their FAQs page, we've set up the table above to compare your total cost of producing eight cane reeds (representing approximately two months worth of cane reeds) with the purchase price of one Legere Reed. Notice that the table is receiving your "Total Cost" number as determined in the first table and multiplying it by an interval of however many reeds you think you'd be replacing with the Legere Reed over a period of several months. It's currently set for 8 reeds but can be adjusted to however many cane reeds a player would be using through the life expectancy of the Legere Reed. Simply change the number in the input field and watch the table update.
With my traditional reed production cost calculated above at $29.73, I found that the Legere Reed only needs a life expectancy of 6 cane reeds in order to justify its $150 price point. If I were to switch "Cold Turkey" over to the Legere Reed and use it for all my playing (discontinue making cane reeds altogether), I'd be saving $87 every two months assuming the synthetic reed only lasts around eight weeks. It's important to remember that Legere claims that the reed will last "several months" so it will likely last much longer than 8 weeks, therein providing even more savings than outlined above.
It's very possible that one might not feel comfortable using the synthetic reed for high profile public performances. So let's say that a player is only looking to practice on the reed at home and play it in some orchestra tuttis or pops concerts, thereby saving their favorite cane reeds for their higher profile playing engagements. Assuming a full-time playing schedule (7 or 8 two to three hour services a week), one is likely producing around 11 professional quality cane reeds every two months to be played in the public, while still needing somewhere around another 5 reeds or so for at home practice. Based on the table above, making 16 cane reeds would cost me $475.68 whereas making 8 cane reeds and purchasing a Legere Reed to cover the remaining 8 needed reeds would only cost me $387.84 a savings of $87.84 every two months by just using the Legere Reed for at home practice and orchestra tuttis/pop concerts, etc.
Even with this extremely conservative usage scenario, the Legere Reed still is saving the player around $90 every few months versus it's traditional cane reed alternatives. If one toggles up the labor costs in the first table to something above minimum wage (as one's time is likely worth much more than minimum wage) margins only increase and much, much more savings are accrued by practicing on the Legere. It's also important to consider the added benefit of the avoidance of repetitive stress injuries to one's neck, back, and hands as we get older that are likely avoided by "outsourcing" 50% of one's weekly reed-making to Legere.

"Conclusion..."

Worst case scenario, if the Legere Reed is functional but you don't feel comfortable playing it for high profile performances, and it only lasts for the absolute minimum of its supposed life expectancy, you are still saving around $90 per purchase versus what you'd be spending making those "good but not great" reeds out of cane. After looking at the dollars and cents, it's getting harder and harder to envision a scenario where even the most skeptical reed-makers value their time and money so little that they would continue on a crusade against a functional synthetic alternative.
Bottom line, if you're getting a green number in the second table above and you're still not purchasing a Legere Reed, you're passing up an opportunity where you would be compromising very little to nothing artistically, while also saving around $90 every few months along with several hours of your time.
